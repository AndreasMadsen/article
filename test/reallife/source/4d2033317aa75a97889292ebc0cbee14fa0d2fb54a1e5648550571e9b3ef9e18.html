4.24pm BST

Airport protest

AP notesx Erdogan's "less belligerent language" in his press conference. It says is unlikely to calm anger as protesters gather at Istanbul's airport to greet him.

Erdogan acknowledged that some Turks have been involved in the protests out of environmental concerns, but insisted that terrorist groups are involved in the unrest in a country seen as a democratic model for the Middle East.

It appeared unlikely that Erdogan's comments would calm demonstrators. Protesters were gathering at an airport in Istanbul in anticipation of the prime minister's return home Thursday night.

Erdogan frequently spends weekends in Istanbul, rather than the capital Ankara. It was unclear whether he chose to land in Istanbul because he has a strong support base going back to his days as the city's mayor.

In Tunisia, Erdogan said he had "love and respect" for protesters with environmental concerns.

"But as I told you earlier, some terrorist groups are involved," he said, claiming that an outlawed left-wing militant group that carried out a suicide bomb attack on the US Embassy in Ankara in February was also involved in the protests.

"They are involved. They have been caught in the streets and on social media," he said.

Erdogan said the Islamic-rooted government had already apologised for the violent police crackdown on the Taksim sit-in, but insisted that tear gas was used everywhere in the world to break up protests.

"Demands cannot be made through illegal means," he said. Erdogan has insisted that democracy happens only at the ballot box, dismissing the demonstrators as an extremist fringe.

Erdogan indicated that the government would go ahead with the redevelopment plans in central Istanbul, insisting the plan would beautify the city, and would include the planting of trees and the construction of a modern theater and opera. Erdogan has earlier said the plans included the construction of a shopping mall.

Some protesters in Taksim Square were skeptical of Erdogan's pledges.

"I do not believe his sincerity," said protester Hazer Berk Buyukturca.

4.14pm BST

Summary

Here's a summary of the latest developments:

• Turkey's prime minister Recep Tayyip Erdogan has continued to defy protesters by condemning some of their "burn and destroy" tactics and pledging to forge ahead with plans for an Istanbul park which triggered the unrest. Speaking on a visit to Tunisia, Erdogan said "terror groups" were manipulating what had started as an environmental campaign, and added that seven foreigners were among those arrested.

• Protesters in Gezi Park have vow to continue their occupation of the space and dismissed Erdogan's claims that the protest was foreign inspired. Ayca Malkop, a26-year-old protester, told the Guardian: "We will still be protesting here no matter what happens."

• Bloomberg reports that stock markets fell after Erdogan's remarks. Themain Istanbul stock index was down almost 5%.

• A police officer has died after falling into an underpass while trying to subdue a protest in southern Turkey, bringing the death toll in a week of protests to three. Governor Huseyin Avni Cos said the officer died in a hospital Thursday after falling into the underpass that was still under construction in Adana, on the Mediterranean coast, the previous night. He was trying to break up protests there.

• Turkish police clashed with demonstrators overnight before Erdogan's return to a country rattled by a week of protest against his leadership. Erdogan returns from a visit to north Africa to face demands he apologise over a fierce police crackdown and sack those who ordered it, following six days of protests that have left two dead and more than 4,000 injured in a dozen cities. Riot police fired teargas at hundreds of demonstrators who threw stones at them and chanted anti-Erdoğan slogans in the heart of the capital, Ankara, on Wednesday night, witnesses said.

3.43pm BST

Gezi Park

Protesters in Istanbul’s Gezi Park have vowed to continue occupying the space and challenged Erdogan’s claim that this is a foreign conspiracy against him.

Speaking from the park, the Guardian’s Luke Harding said:

Protesters are nonplussed by the prime minister’s latest remarks that this is all a foreign plot against him. They say it is no such thing – it is a spontaneous grassroots movement, and that’s certainly what it feels like.

Luke then handed the phone to 26-year-old protester Ayca Malkop who predicted a big protest tonight. Speaking on a poor line she insisted that the protesters were Turkish and not guided by external forces. Although the movement welcomed supporters from outside the country, she said: “People are not forcing us to protest.”

When Malkop was told of Erdogan’s pledge to go ahead with the park redevelopment, she added:

We need green space. This is a very important space for us. I don’t think this news is good for us now. We will still be protesting here no matter what happens.

Luke said the park was like the Glastonbury festival.

It’s an amazing scene of people drumming, people dancing, there are slogans everywhere, many of them against the prime minister, Mr Erdogan, who is arriving back in Istanbul tonight. But others which are ecological, socialist, pro-alcohol. It a massively colourful and variegated scene with kebab stalls and watermelons.

3.12pm BST

Softer tone?

Erdogan was more conciliatory than his remarks first appeared, but he did vow to go ahead with redevelopment of the park, according to Hurriyet Daily News.

Reuters has this write-up of Erdogan's press conference and the reaction to it on the markets.

Erdogan condemned the "burn and destroy" tactics of some of those involved in days of violent protests on Thursday, and promised to press ahead with plans for an Istanbul park which triggered the unrest.

Speaking on a visit to Tunisia, Erdogan said "terror groups" were manipulating what had started as an environmental campaign, and added that seven foreigners were among those arrested.

"If you say: 'I will hold a meeting and burn and destroy,' we will not allow that," he told reporters after meeting his Tunisian counterpart. "We are against the majority dominating the minority and we cannot tolerate the opposite."

By confining his comments to a group of protesters, Erdogan appeared softer in tone than before he left for north Africa at the start of the week, when he described the demonstrators in blanket terms as looters.

Nevertheless, his defiance rattled nervous financial markets. The main Istanbul stock index was down 4.7% by 12.57 GMT while the lira weakened to 1.8923 against the dollar. The two-year benchmark bond yield rose to its highest in more than six months.

Updated at 4.02pm BST

2.42pm BST

Reaction to Erdogan

Here's a roundup of some initial reaction to Erdogan's remarks.

The Istanbul-based analyst Aaron Stein, author of the blog Turkey Wonk, notes that Erdogan's defiance runs counter to the conciliatory tone of his deputy, Bulent Arinc, and the president, Abdullah Gul.

Mahir Zeynalov, from Today's Zaman, says Erdogan's tone was different but he couldn't bring himself to change course.

The Turkey analyst Ihan Tanir suggests the ruling party will now change tack.

CNN's Ivan Watson says Erdogan's remarks may have been mistranslated.

Summary

We are resuming live coverage of events in Turkey following Prime Minister Recep Tayyip Erdogan's defiant press conference in Tunis.

Here's a quick summary of the latest developments:

• Erdogan vowed to go ahead with the redevelopment of Istanbul’s Taksim Square, defying angry protesters who continue to rally against the plan, al-Arabiya reports. Speaking to reporters in Tunis after a meeting with his Tunisian counterpart, Erdogan urged protesters to abide by the law and demonstrate in designated areas, dismissing them as “extremists” and saying everything would calm down before he returned.

He was trying to break up protests there.
