Malaysia Leader Names Cabinet

KUALA LUMPUR—Malaysia’s Prime Minister Najib Razak unveiled a new Cabinet on Wednesday with a mix of technocrats, including the chief executive of the largest local bank, and career politicians, as he sought to balance experience with new ideas and vowed to fight graft.

But the scant Chinese representation in the Cabinet risks polarizing the multiethnic country and complicates Mr. Najib’s efforts to win back tens of thousands of urban voters who deserted the ruling National Front coalition in the May 5 election.

In a televised announcement, Mr. Najib named 30 Cabinet ministers that included Paul Low, the sole Chinese member who is an anticorruption campaigner and president of the Malaysian chapter of the global watchdog Transparency International. Other professionals include Abdul Wahid Omar, the chief executive of Malayan Banking Bhd, the largest Malaysian bank by assets.

Still, more than half of the cabinet is made up of appointees from United Malays National Organization, the core at 13-party ruling coalition that has ruled Malaysia since it gained independence from Britain in 1957. The remainder are allotted to other coalition partners.

The latest general elections dealt the ruling coalition its worst results ever. Mr. Najib retained power with the thinnest victory margin for the National Front. The coalition won 60% of the seats in Parliament, but cornered only 47% of the popular vote, as the opposition’s fierce campaign to eradicate corruption found widespread support among urban voters and helped it secure 50% of the vote.

Mr. Najib called the heavy loss in popular support a “Chinese tsunami.”

“Now it is time for all of us, in government and beyond, to put the bitterness behind us, and work towards national reconciliation—and national unity,” Mr. Najib said.

The main ethnic Chinese coalition partner, the Malaysian Chinese Association, decided not to take any Cabinet berth after its dismal showing at the poll.

Analysts say this is hurting Mr. Najib’s efforts for suitable representation of the community, which makes up about a quarter of Malaysia’s 28 million population and are often wealthier than the majority ethnic Malays.

The Chinese have been upset with the UMNO’s policy that favors ethnic Malays in everything from education to government jobs. Thousands leave the country to seek better prospects abroad in a chronic brain drain that the World Bank says could hurt Malaysia’s ambition of reducing the wealth gap between richer Asian economies such as Singapore and South Korea by 2020.

“The National Front is in trouble because of their race-based parties,” said Ooi Kee Beng, deputy director at Singapore’s Institute of Southeast Asian Studies. “The composition of the Cabinet partly comes from tradition under the coalition-based politics and this will have political consequences by making it very difficult for the National Front to continue to talk about racial politics going forward,” he said.

Race has long dominated Malaysia’s politics. But, the British-educated Mr. Najib has been chipping away at such race-based preferences as he aims to boost meritocracy.

“The new cabinet will be responsible for delivering transformation in Malaysia and continuing the economic and political reforms that began four years ago,” Mr. Najib said.

Mr. Najib will be aided by Muhyiddin Yassin as deputy prime minister and Ahmad Husni Hanadzlah as the second finance minister. The four, along with Mustapa Mohamad as the international trade and industry minister, will steer the bulk of Malaysia’s economic management and policy planning.

They face an economy that is slowing amid sputtering global demand for the country’s exports. Malaysia announced Wednesday that its economy grew 4.1% from a year earlier—its slowest growth in more than three years—decelerating from a revised 6.5% expansion in the 2012 fourth quarter and below market expectations of 5% growth.

The defense and education portfolios are senior positions traditionally reserved for top leaders in the UMNO party.

Hishammuddin Hussein was appointed as defense minister while Mr. Muhyiddin will lead the education ministry. Both Mr. Najib and his predecessor Abdullah Ahmad Badawi have served in the two positions.
